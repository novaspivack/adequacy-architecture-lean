\begin{abstract}
Outer certificates --- compressed witnesses, abstraction layers, quotient
representations, transport claims, upgrade witnesses --- appear in almost
every domain where rich internal structure must be represented through an
external predicate.  A fundamental question about these certificates is
almost never asked directly: do the different routes to outer admissibility
define different worlds, or do they collapse to one?  And if they collapse,
what is the architecture above the collapsed gate?

This paper proves that, in a broad and disciplined certificate world, outer
admissibility is not a flat property.  It has a forced architecture.
The apparent plurality of outer admissibility routes collapses to a single
unified gate, $\Gadm \equiv \Upb$.  That gate implies the existence of an
absolute frontier certificate, is maximal under a declared grammar of outer
normal forms, and is stable against the nearest boundary candidate.  Above
the gate, a richer second-layer package is canonically forced without new
hypotheses.  And stronger branch-specific content sits above both levels as
a residual band, partially reconstructible but irreducibly richer than the
gate alone.

The result is a strict three-level architecture:
\emph{one outer gate of admission, one functorial second layer, one residual
band.}
Every boundary is characterized by a named, machine-checked theorem.  All
proofs are in Lean~4 with 0 \texttt{sorry}.  The Lean library contains over
225 named formal objects across the outer gate program and its foundational
base.  Exact theorem names and modules are given in the companion note and
appendix.
\end{abstract}

\section{The problem}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{The phenomenon}

Formal and applied systems routinely rely on outer certificates --- external
or interface-level predicates that claim to stand in for richer internal
structure.  These take many forms: \emph{compressed witnesses} that claim
to capture essential proof content, \emph{abstraction layers} that claim
to preserve enough structure for downstream reasoning, \emph{quotient and
interface representations} that project internal distinctions into a coarser
type, \emph{transport claims} that assert a structure moved along a
reindexing retains its certificate content, and \emph{upgrade witnesses}
that present a certified object at one level as certifying a richer level.

In every case, the outer predicate makes the same claim: ``the relevant
content is here, at this interface, in this form.''  Without a theory of
outer admissibility, such certificates are under-classified and often
over-trusted: a pullback display, a branch-native pack, and an upgrade
witness appear to be three different kinds of outer certificate defining
three different admissibility worlds.

The central discovery of this paper is that \emph{they are not}.  They are
forced presentations of a single outer gate.  Richer content does not
spread sideways into alternative primitive modes --- it is forced upward
into a layered architecture above the gate.  That is the architectural
fact this paper proves.

\subsection{The adequacy burden}

The Adequacy Architecture formalizes a prior and more fundamental constraint
on this question.  Before asking \emph{which} outer certificates are honest,
one must understand what it means for any account to be adequate.

The core insight, proved as a machine-checked theorem, is:

\begin{keybox}[title=The fundamental theorem (informal)]
  If a system is rich enough, adequacy has a cost.  You cannot freely
  flatten, summarize, certify, or route around the burden of adequacy
  without that burden reappearing somewhere else.
\end{keybox}

This is not a philosophical claim.  Four proved consequences build the
foundation on which the outer admissibility theorems rest:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No free adequate collapse.}
    If a representation is adequate in a non-trivial mode, it cannot erase
    all burden without paying elsewhere~\cite{SpivackAdequacy}.

  \item \textbf{Burden relocates; it does not vanish.}
    Honest simplification moves structural obligations to another layer or
    carrier.  The obligations are not deleted; they are relocated.

  \item \textbf{Route architecture is forced.}
    Once adequacy and burden are genuinely entangled, route and finality
    claims become part of what must be exhibited.  They cannot be
    treated as decorative.

  \item \textbf{Every forgetful morphism has a residual story.}
    Any map that loses information has a canonical residual structure
    recording what was lost, where it went, and what still observes it.
    This is the machine-checked form of the diagnostic principle:
    if the burden is invisible, suspect the map.
\end{enumerate}

These four facts are not background.  They are the structural reason that
outer certificate worlds cannot be flat: adequacy forces burden, burden
forces route architecture, and maps that compress structure have residuals
that cannot be erased.  The outer gate theorem is the culmination of this
chain for the specific world of typed outer certificate predicates.

\subsection{The classification problem}

Given the prior foundation, the outer classification problem becomes precise.

An outer certificate predicate $C : \mathsf{Type} \to \mathsf{Prop}$
evaluated at a type-indexed slot $\gamma$ claims: ``this slot is genuinely
admissible --- it connects to the frontier of the adequacy landscape.''
There are many ways such a claim could be made: via pullback displays,
via full branch-specific packs, via upgrade witnesses, via grammar-generated
composites.

The natural question is whether these are genuinely different admissibility
conditions, or presentations of the same one.  But that question leads
immediately to three more:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \textbf{Do admissibility routes unify?}
    Are different presentations of admission actually the same gate?
  \item \textbf{What is forced above the gate?}
    Does admission unlock richer structure canonically, or only with
    additional hypotheses?
  \item \textbf{What is irreducibly residual?}
    Is there branch-specific content that is stronger than the gate but
    cannot be absorbed into it or into the second layer?
\end{enumerate}

This paper answers all three questions with a single architectural fact:

\begin{keybox}
\centering
\emph{Apparently different outer certificate forms do not define different
admissibility worlds.  Under disciplined adequacy constraints, they collapse
to one outer gate, force a derived higher layer above it, and leave stronger
branch-specific content in a residual band that cannot be absorbed into
either level.}
\end{keybox}

\section{Why this is hard and important}
\label{sec:importance}

\subsection{The na\"{\i}ve answer fails}

A na\"{\i}ve answer to the outer classification problem would be: any
predicate that happens to hold at some $\gamma$ counts as admissible.
That answer is empty --- it makes the notion of admissibility meaningless.

A better but still na\"{\i}ve answer would be: enumerate the known
certificate constructors and call each of them admissible.  But this
\emph{pluralist} answer raises its own problem: it produces a collection of
admissibility conditions without answering whether they form one class or
many, what their relationship is, and where the ceiling is.

\subsection{What makes the problem non-trivial}

The genuine difficulty is this.

From the supply side: the finite admissibility family $\Gadm$ is defined
by three structurally different constructor arms.  On the surface, these
look like three different kinds of certificates:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A pullback display along a compare map.
  \item A full pack of conditions connecting the infinity-compression
    architecture to the standard carrier.
  \item A certified upgrade witness from the representation calculus.
\end{enumerate}
There is no obvious reason these should be the same.

From the demand side: any unified gate must have content.
It cannot be vacuous.  The witness $\Upb$ requires a genuine certified
frontier representation obtained by pulling back a lawful adequacy
architecture along a compare map.  This in turn requires a non-trivial
compression carrier pullback (through the infinity-compression arm) or
an involutive compare-map cleaving argument (through the carrier-swap arm),
each proved as a separate machine-checked theorem~\cite{SpivackAdequacy}.

\subsection{What the architecture really shows}

The deeper import of this program is not taxonomic.  It is that outer
certificate worlds are \emph{not free}.  They do not consist of independent
admissibility conditions that one can mix and match.  They have a forced
architecture, and the forcing is provable.

The governed class is broad: it covers outer certificates built from pullback
displays along compare maps, finite admissibility presentations with multiple
arms, grammar-generated classes under declared normal forms, boundary forms
that are sound but outside the grammar, functorial consequences of admission,
and residual branch-specific packs above both levels.  The theorem stack
answers exactly where each kind fits and what it can and cannot do.

Three consequences make this concrete.

\textbf{The outer gate is the unique admissibility ceiling.}  Once the
normal-form grammar is fixed, no class generated from it can exceed $\Upb$:
the gate is not the highest thing found, it is the highest thing there is
within the grammar (\S\ref{sec:maximality}).

\textbf{The second layer cannot be reached from below.}  No admissibility
route produces the second-layer content directly; it is produced
canonically \emph{after} admission, with no new hypotheses --- what the
gate forces into existence above itself (\S\ref{sec:second-layer}).

\textbf{The residual content cannot be absorbed.}  The branch-specific
residual condition carries a genuine irreducible piece that the outer gate
alone cannot supply, blocking its absorption into either level
(\S\ref{sec:residual}).

When someone presents an outer predicate as an admissibility condition,
the architecture asks four questions whose answers determine its position
in the forced three-level architecture: does it imply an absolute frontier
certificate (gate consequence); is it grammar-generated (ceiling test);
does it unlock a second layer or is it second-layer content (layering
test); is it primitive or residual (residuality test)?

\section{The central discovery}
\label{sec:thesis}

\subsection{What the theorems say}

Before the formal machinery, the central picture can be stated plainly.

\begin{keybox}[title=Central discovery]
Outer certificate worlds are not merely plural collections of admissibility
tests.  They are \textbf{forced into a layered architecture} with one
unified gate, derived higher structure, and residual stronger content.
Specifically:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item There is a \textbf{single unified outer gate of honest admission}.
    Multiple finite admissibility presentations --- pullback display, full
    branch pack, upgrade witness --- collapse to one gate $\Gadm \equiv \Upb$.
  \item The unified gate \textbf{has a real consequence}: it implies the
    existence of an absolute frontier certificate.
  \item The gate is \textbf{maximal under the declared grammar}
    (NF0--NF3): no grammar-generated class exceeds it, and the nearest
    non-generated boundary candidate does not force expansion.
  \item Above the gate, a \textbf{functorial second layer} is canonically
    produced from the same witness, without additional hypotheses.  It is
    not a new admissibility mode; it is what admission \emph{unlocks}.
  \item \textbf{Stronger branch-specific content is genuinely residual}:
    it does not collapse into Level~1 or Level~2, but carries its own
    irreducible obstruction.
\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Three forced levels, not three choices}

The five points above are not a tidy taxonomy.  They are a structural
impossibility result.  The three levels are not choices.  They are three
different kinds of structural content, each forced into its own band by
the logic of admissibility: the grammar-cut maximality theorem blocks
escape from Level~1 by composition, the layering theorem blocks direct
access to Level~2 from below, and the residuality result blocks absorption
of Level~3 into either level above.

The three-level architecture:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\textbf{Level 1} & Outer gate: $\Gadm \equiv \Upb$ (one gate, grammar-maximal) \\
\textbf{Level 2} & Functorial consequence: $\Upb \Rightarrow \Stwo$ (derived, not primitive) \\
\textbf{Residual} & Branch-specific content $\StageD$ (irreducibly above both levels) \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Every boundary is characterized by a named machine-checked theorem.  The
boundaries are not informal: they are defined by what does and does not
follow from $\Upb$ alone.

\section{The formal world and disciplined scope}
\label{sec:scope}

\subsection{The type-indexed outer world}

Fix a universe of types.  An \emph{outer certificate predicate} is a
predicate $C : \mathsf{Type} \to \mathsf{Prop}$.  We evaluate such
predicates at a type-indexed slot $\gamma$.

\begin{definition}[Finite admissibility gate --- $\Gadm$]
  $\Gadm\,\gamma$ (\lean{Program1FiniteGAdm}) holds when $\gamma$ is
  witnessed by one of three constructor arms:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item A pullback display arm:
      $\lean{Program1AdmissibilityPullbackDisplayWitness}\,\gamma$.
    \item A branch-native full pack arm (connecting the
      infinity-compression architecture to the standard carrier):
      $\lean{Program1IcStageDFullPackWitness}\,\gamma$.
    \item A representation-calculus upgrade witness arm:
      $\lean{Program1CertifiedUpgradeWitness}\,\gamma$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Pullback display witness --- $\Upb$]
  $\Upb\,\gamma$ (\lean{Program1Admissibility\-PullbackDisplayWitness}) holds
  when there exists a certified frontier representation obtained by
  pulling back a lawful adequacy architecture along a compare map at
  $\gamma$.
\end{definition}

$\Upb$ is not vacuous.  The branch-native arm requires a non-trivial
compression carrier pullback.  The carrier-swap arm requires an
involutive compare-map cleaving argument.  Both are proved as separate
machine-checked theorems~\cite{SpivackAdequacy}.

\subsection{The outer normal-form grammar}

The outer grammar is the closure of the following four normal forms under
declared grammar rules:
\begin{description}
  \item[NF0.] Bare pullback display.
  \item[NF1.] Certified frontier row.
  \item[NF2.] Upgrade witness.
  \item[NF3.] Composition of the above.
\end{description}
$\Ggen\,C$ means $C : \mathsf{Type} \to \mathsf{Prop}$ is
generated by this grammar.  NF4 ($\SoneLawful$), the lawful S1 frontier
row predicate, lies outside the grammar.  The maximality theorem
(\S\ref{sec:maximality}) shows that nothing grammar-generated exceeds
the outer gate, and the NF4 boundary analysis shows that even this
nearest non-generated candidate does not force expansion.

The outer gate program builds on two prior machine-checked results.
First, the highest reachable summit $H_{\mathrm{final}}$ is packaged as a

  \item The NF4 boundary analysis is a boundary report, not a completeness

  \item The residual band (\S\ref{sec:residual}) is branch-specific.  Its

\section{Collapse to the outer gate}
\label{sec:gate}

\subsection{The unification theorem}

The first major result shows that the apparent plurality of admissibility
routes collapses.

\begin{theorem}[Unification]
\label{thm:unification}
  For all $\gamma$,\quad $\Gadm\,\gamma \iff \Upb\,\gamma$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{leanbox}
program1FiniteGAdm_iff_program1AdmissibilityPullbackDisplayWitness :
  forall gamma, G_adm gamma <-> U_pullback gamma
-- Module: Instances/Program1MetaUnificationMU1.lean
\end{leanbox}

This is nontrivial in both directions.

\emph{Three arms collapse (the non-trivial direction).}  Each of the three
constructor arms of $\Gadm$ must be shown to produce a $\Upb$ witness:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The pullback arm is immediate (first constructor).
  \item The branch-native full pack arm threads through a non-trivial
    compression carrier pullback (the infinity-compression compare-map
    representation, proved separately in the Lean library).
  \item The upgrade witness arm factors through the certified summit-row
    representation via the representation-calculus gate.
\end{itemize}

\emph{The gate is not empty (the honesty direction).}  $\Upb$ requires a
genuine lawful adequacy architecture pulled back along a compare map at
$\gamma$.  Four concrete witnesses have been constructed --- using the
infinity-compression pullback, an identity-level corpus instantiation, a
carrier-swap corpus instantiation, and a parity quotient on a three-element
type --- each requiring distinct non-trivial arguments~\cite{SpivackAdequacy}.

  For all $\gamma$,\quad $\Gadm\,\gamma \Rightarrow \exists P\,B,\,

\end{theorem}

\end{theorem}

  forall (C : Type -> Prop),
    Program1GrammarGeneratedOuterGate C ->
    forall gamma, C gamma -> U_pullback gamma
-- Module: Instances/Program1OuterGateGrammar.lean

Theorem~\ref{thm:maximality} establishes that $\Upb$ is the \emph{ceiling}
of the declared grammar.  Any outer predicate generated by composing,
lifting, or grammar-producing from NF0--NF3 stays within $\Upb$.

Without this theorem, the unified gate would be a lower bound: something
that everything we know satisfies, but which could in principle be exceeded
by some more expressive grammar-generated class we have not tried.  With it,
the gate is tight: within the declared scope, there is no room above it.

The nearest principled candidate for a broader outer class is the NF4
predicate $\SoneLawful\,\gamma$, which asserts the non-emptiness of a
lawful S1 frontier row at $\gamma$.

\label{thm:nf4-sound}
  For all $\gamma$,\quad $\SoneLawful\,\gamma \Rightarrow
  \lean{program1OuterLayerBLaw}\,\gamma$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{leanbox}

\end{enumerate}

NF4 therefore remains a logical pocket: sound where instantiated, but not
promoted to the operative outer gate.

\subsection{Counterexample: S1-shape is necessary}

Before stating what the NF4 boundary means, we note that an explicit

There exist explicit pairs $(P, B)$ where no lawful S1 frontier row exists.
This means the S1-shape requirement in the architecture is load-bearing:
it is not trivially satisfied.  The grammar boundary separates something
real from something vacuous.

\subsection{What the NF4 boundary means}

The NF4 finding matters in two directions simultaneously.

\emph{Downward:} it confirms the architecture is not trivially
all-encompassing.  The grammar boundary is real.  There exist predicates
(like $\SoneLawful$) that are not grammar-generated and not universally
equal to $\Upb$.

\emph{Upward:} NF4 did not force expansion.  The nearest candidate for
``maybe there is a broader class'' turned out not to yield one.  The
architecture is stable at its current boundary --- not because we could not
find anything stronger, but because the nearest candidate was examined and
did not fit.

Together, Theorems~\ref{thm:maximality} and~\ref{thm:nf4-sound} give a
rigorous sense in which $\Upb$ is the right outer gate at the current level
of structure: maximal in the declared grammar, and stable against the
nearest boundary test.

\section{The second layer above admission}
\label{sec:second-layer}

\subsection{The second-layer consequence}

One of the most structurally important results is not about the outer gate
itself.  It is about what the outer gate unlocks.

\begin{theorem}[Functorial second layer]
\label{thm:second-layer}
  For all $\gamma$,\quad $\Upb\,\gamma \;\Rightarrow\; \Stwo\,\gamma$
  (and therefore $\Gadm\,\gamma \;\Rightarrow\; \Stwo\,\gamma$).
\end{theorem}

\begin{leanbox}
program1_layer2PullbackDisplay_with_host_summitFE3_of_U_pullback :
  forall gamma, U_pullback gamma -> L2 gamma
program1_layer2PullbackDisplay_with_host_summitFE3_of_program1FiniteGAdm :
  forall gamma, G_adm gamma -> L2 gamma
-- Module: Instances/Program1LayeredStructureLS1.lean
\end{leanbox}

The $\Stwo$ package (\lean{pullbackDisplay\_with\_host\_summitFE3}) combines
the pullback display with a host-side summit certificate.  It is
obtained by projecting from the same witness that gives $\Upb\,\gamma$
--- no additional hypotheses are required.

\subsection{Why this is structurally important}

$\Stwo$ is not a new constructor of $\Gadm$, not grammar-generated, and
does not imply $\Gadm$ --- it is downstream.  It is canonically produced
from any $\Upb$ witness without choice or additional hypotheses.

This means: an outer certificate is not just a stamp of approval.  It is
an entry ticket to a richer world.  Admission is not a dead end; it is the
beginning of a richer theory.  The second-layer package combines the
pullback display with a host-side summit certificate, giving a richer
simultaneous view than admission alone provides.  The outer certificate
world has strict vertical structure: Level~1 (admission) below, Level~2
(its canonical functorial consequence) above.  These are not the same level,
and nothing in Level~2 can be accessed without first passing through Level~1.

\section{Stage D and the residual band}
\label{sec:residual}

\subsection{What the residual condition is}

The residual condition $\StageD$ is a branch-specific pack of requirements
connecting the infinity-compression architecture to the highest reachable
summit at the standard carrier.  It decomposes into four conjuncts:

\begin{theorem}[Residual condition decomposition]
\label{thm:staged-d}
  For all $\gamma$,
  \[
    \StageD\,\gamma
    \;\iff\;
    F_1(\gamma) \;\wedge\; F_2(\gamma) \;\wedge\; F_3(\gamma) \;\wedge\; F_4(\gamma)
  \]
  where $F_1 =$ obstruction entry, $F_2 =$ absolute-frontier S1
  conjunct, $F_3 =$ bypass, $F_4 =$ cascade.
\end{theorem}

Each of the four conjuncts has a named machine-checked lemma (see the
companion note for exact names).  Facets $F_1$, $F_3$, $F_4$ are
reconstructible from named hypotheses.  $F_2$ is the irreducible residue.

\subsection{The residuality result}

\begin{proposition}[Residual condition residuality]
\label{prop:sr-b}
  \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item $\Upb\,\gamma$ does not imply $\StageD\,\gamma$ in general.
    \item $\StageD\,\gamma$ does not imply $\Upb\,\gamma$ without the $F_1$
      and bypass arms.
    \item $F_1$, $F_3$, $F_4$ are reconstructible from named hypotheses.
    \item $F_2$ (the absolute-frontier S1 conjunct) is the irreducible
      residue: it does not follow from $\Upb\,\gamma$ alone.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Why residuality matters}

Without this residuality result, one might conclude: ``the residual
condition is just another outer admissibility condition; we should add it
to the gate.''  Proposition~\ref{prop:sr-b} closes this off decisively.
$\StageD$ is not primitive to the outer gate.  It is not part of the
second layer.  It is branch-specific extra structure with its own internal
organization (the four conjuncts) and its own irreducible obstruction
($F_2$).  The genuinely stronger branch-specific conditions do not sneak
back into Level~1 or Level~2 --- they live in their own residual band,
partially accessible via named lemmas, with an irreducible core.

The existence of a residual band is not a failure of the architecture;
it is a finding.  The residual band is the boundary between ``what the
outer gate governs'' and ``what requires more specific internal structure
to access.''  The architecture does not claim that $\Upb$ governs
everything --- it claims that $\Upb$ is the right outer gate at its level
of structure, and that what lies beyond is classified rather than ignored.

The general principle underlying this finding is machine-checked throughout
the architecture: every forgetful morphism has a canonical fundamental
residual structure recording what was lost and what still observes it.
$F_2$ is the specific instance of this principle for the
infinity-compression branch: the map from the outer gate to the
compression architecture forgets exactly the $F_2$ content.

\subsection{The integrated theorem}

\begin{keybox}[title=Main theorem of the outer admissibility program]
For the disciplined outer admissibility world:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(I) Collapse.}\quad All finite admissibility presentations
--- the pullback display arm, the branch-native full pack arm, the
upgrade witness arm --- are logically equivalent: $\Gadm \equiv \Upb$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:unification}).  There is one outer gate.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(II) Consequence.}\quad The outer gate has a real payoff:
$\Gadm\,\gamma \Rightarrow \exists P\,B,\,\AbsRawSone(P,B)$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:frontier}).  Admission implies an absolute frontier
certificate exists.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(III) Maximality.}\quad The outer gate is the ceiling of
the declared grammar: no predicate generated from NF0--NF3 exceeds $\Upb$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:maximality}).  The nearest boundary candidate (NF4) does
not force expansion: the grammar boundary is real and stable
(\S\ref{sec:maximality}).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(IV) Forced second layer.}\quad A richer second-layer
package $\Stwo$ is canonically produced from any admission witness, with
no additional hypotheses (Theorem~\ref{thm:second-layer}).  The second
layer is not a new admissibility mode; it is what admission forces into
existence above itself.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(V) Residual band.}\quad Stronger branch-specific content
$\StageD$ is not primitive to the outer gate, is not part of the second
layer, and carries a genuine irreducible obstruction
(Proposition~\ref{prop:sr-b}).  It is classified as residual, not
dismissed.
\end{keybox}

\noindent
Points I--III together say: the outer gate is the unique correct gate ---
not the highest thing found, but the highest thing provably reachable
within the declared grammar.  Point IV says: the gate is not a dead end
but the entrance to a forced richer world.  Point V says: the richer
branch-specific content is accounted for but does not collapse the gate.

Together these five points characterize the full forced structure of outer
admissibility: \emph{one gate, one derived layer, one residual band.}
The architecture could not have come out flatter.  Every attempt to flatten
it --- to conflate the levels, to absorb the residual, to escape the gate
by grammar composition --- is blocked by a proved theorem.  The three
levels are the minimum that the logic of admissibility forces into existence.

\section{Robustness and breadth of applicability}
\label{sec:robustness}

\subsection{Why the gate is robust}

Four structurally heterogeneous admissibility presentations have already
been shown to collapse to $\Upb$: the infinity-compression pullback arm
(compression carrier along a constant compare map), two carrier-level
corpus instantiations (identity and carrier-swap, the latter via involutive
cleaving), a parity quotient on a three-element type, and the certified
upgrade witness arm.  Each required a distinct proof; none collapses
trivially.

Beyond these instances, grammar-cut maximality says the result holds for
\emph{any} predicate generated by the outer normal-form grammar --- not
just the specific ones proved above, but the entire closure under NF0--NF3.
The gate is therefore not the highest specific case found; it is the highest
provable ceiling within the declared grammar.

\subsection{Honest boundaries}

The architecture does not claim to govern all outer predicates.  NF4 exists,
is sound, and is not grammar-generated --- proving the grammar boundary is
real.  The NF4 finding then provides an independent robustness check: the
nearest candidate for a broader class was examined and did not force
expansion.  The grammar boundary is genuine, not accidental.

Equally, the residual condition is not claimed to be unreachable.  It has a
partial basis and an irreducible residue; it is classified precisely, not
dismissed.  An honest architecture locates its own limits.

\section{Breadth: what this architecture governs}
\label{sec:breadth}

\subsection{What class of certificate worlds is governed}

The architecture governs any certificate world where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item There is a notion of adequacy with real, non-erasable burden.
  \item There is a lawful class of architectures supporting stratified
    adequacy, bottleneck structure, and bridge relationships between layers.
  \item There are outer certificate predicates evaluated at type-indexed
    slots.
  \item There is a grammar of outer normal forms.
\end{itemize}

Within such a world, the entire theorem stack applies: the outer gate
unifies, the second layer is forced above it, and residual content is
classified and located.

More specifically, the architecture governs outer certificate forms built
from any combination of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Pullback-style representability along a compare map.
  \item Finite admissibility presentations with multiple constructor arms.
  \item Grammar-generated admissibility schemes under declared normal forms.
  \item Quotient and interface certificate forms.
  \item Upgrade or witness-promotion routes.
  \item Functorial layered consequence packages.
\end{itemize}

This is not a narrow or idiosyncratic collection.  These are the standard
forms that outer certificates take across formal mathematics, proof
architecture, and structured representation theory.  The theorem stack
therefore applies broadly, not only to the specific instantiation branches
studied here.

The key point is negative as much as positive: \emph{no outer certificate
form built from the declared grammar of normal forms can escape the outer
gate.}  The grammar-cut maximality theorem is a completeness result for
the class of normal-form outer certificates.  Everything built from
NF0--NF3 is accounted for.

The broader significance is this: this paper is not classifying a menu
of admissibility witnesses.  It is proving the \emph{architecture of
outer admissibility itself} --- the forced structure that any sufficiently
disciplined outer certificate world must have.  The three levels are not
three outcomes of one particular construction.  They are three kinds of
structural content that the logic of admissibility forces into three
distinct bands.

\subsection{Three instantiation branches}

The architecture has three concrete instantiation branches:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \emph{halting/semantic} branch (NEMS), which provides
    halting-anchored semantic summit certificates.
  \item The \emph{infinity-compression} branch (IC), which provides
    compression carriers and native stage-D structure.
  \item The \emph{continuation uniformization} branch (APS), which
    provides indexed middle-layer structure via recursion uniformization.
\end{itemize}

Each branch supplies a distinct instantiation route into the outer gate.
The three-branch closure theorem
(\lean{three\_branch\_corpus\_highest\_summit\_at})
shows that all three branches supply the highest reachable summit at the
standard carrier.  The outer gate unification (Theorem~\ref{thm:unification})
then shows that what all three branches contribute collapses to the same
outer gate.

This means the outer gate theorem is not a statement about one branch.
It is a statement about the whole three-branch structure.

The halting/semantic branch carries a full semantic faithfulness story: all
consequences of master-indexed summit agreement --- agreed summit, summit
certificate, indexed certificates, forgetful lifts --- collapse to a single
machine-checked bundle, just as the outer admissibility routes collapse to
a single $\Upb$.

\subsection{Connection to the foundational laws}

The outer admissibility results are not isolated.  Each foundational law
finds its reflection here: no free collapse means the gate requires a
genuine pullback witness; burden relocates means the three arms all
relocate into the same pullback structure; route architecture is forced
means the second layer $\Stwo$ is exactly what admission forces above
itself; and the residual condition's irreducibility ($F_2$) is the
machine-checked form of ``if the burden is invisible, suspect the map.''
The three-level architecture is the outer-certificate expression of a
principle that was present from the foundations.

\section{What lies beyond the summit}
\label{sec:beyond}

The architecture is not claiming to be complete in a global sense.  Three
frontiers are honestly open.

\subsection{Global unconditional frontier}

The existence of an absolute frontier S1 certificate for an arbitrary
predicate--burden pair without hypotheses --- $\AbsRawSone(P,B)$ for all
$P,B$ --- remains open.  This is the next genuine research frontier
beyond the outer gate program~\cite{SpivackAdequacy}.  It is not a missing
piece of the current architecture; it is where the current architecture
points next.

\subsection{Broader outer grammar}

If a principled grammar larger than the current NF0--NF3 normal-form
grammar were ever chartered, the grammar-cut maximality result would need
re-examination in that scope.  The NF4 boundary finding is not a proof that
no broader grammar is possible.  It is a proof that the nearest specific
candidate (NF4) did not force one now.  A future investigation could, in
principle, extend the grammar and re-examine the ceiling.

\subsection{Residual condition universality}

Whether the residual condition $\StageD$ could ever be brought inside the
outer gate for all $\gamma$ --- i.e., whether $\Upb \Rightarrow \StageD$
universally --- or whether the branch-specific obstruction ($F_2$) is
genuinely permanent, is open.  The residuality result gives ``partial
basis, irreducible residue.''  Establishing $\Upb \Rightarrow \StageD$
universally would be a genuine extension of the current theorem stack.

\subsection{What these open frontiers mean}

These three frontiers are not gaps in the current theorem stack.  They are
the next genuine research questions, now clearly located by the architecture.
The architecture does not hide them; it exposes them precisely.

A frontier that is clearly located is more valuable than a frontier that is
obscured by vague language.  The current theorem stack has the virtue of
telling you exactly where the edge is and why.
